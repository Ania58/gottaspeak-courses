## 3. Grammar – Substitution

### one / ones

- I don’t like this sentence, I prefer the other one  
- These examples are clearer than the previous ones  

---

### do / does / did

- She translated it well, and I did too  
- He didn’t explain it clearly, and she didn’t either  

---

### so / such

- I think this works, and I hope so  
- It was such a difficult sentence  

---

## 4. Build Substitution

Rewrite without repeating words:

1. This translation is better than that translation  
2. I like this version more than that version  
3. These sentences are clearer than those sentences  
4. This example is easier than the previous example  
5. That phrase sounds more natural than this phrase

## 9. Reading

Read the text.

One of the most common problems in language learning is repetition. Learners often repeat the same words and structures, which can make their speech sound unnatural and limited.

In natural communication, speakers use a variety of strategies to avoid repetition. Instead of repeating the same nouns, they use substitution, referring back to ideas in a more efficient way.

For example, instead of repeating a full phrase, a speaker may use words like "one", "ones" or "do". This allows the message to remain clear while sounding more natural.

In translation, avoiding repetition is especially important. A sentence that repeats too much may sound unnatural, even if it is technically correct. What matters is not only accuracy, but also how the text flows.
