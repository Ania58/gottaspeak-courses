# Lesson 41 – News and Headlines

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:
- understand how headlines influence interpretation  
- recognise exaggeration and emotional language  
- compare different ways of presenting the same information  

---

## 1. Warm-up

Discuss:

1. Do you usually read full articles or just headlines?
2. Can headlines be misleading?
3. Why do headlines often sound dramatic?
4. Have you ever misunderstood a news story because of a headline?

---

## 2. Headline vs Reality

Read the headlines.

1. “City in Chaos After Minor Incident”  
2. “Experts Warn of ‘Disaster’ in Education System”  
3. “Miracle Product Changes Lives Overnight”  
4. “New Study Shocks Scientists”  

---

### Task

For each headline:

👉 What impression does it create?  
👉 What might the real situation be?  

---

## 3. Spot the Technique

Match each headline to the technique used.

Techniques:
a) exaggeration  
b) emotional language  
c) vagueness  
d) sensationalism  

Headlines:

1. “Terrifying Situation Develops Overnight”  
2. “Something Big Is Happening”  
3. “Crisis Hits Local Community”  
4. “You Won’t Believe What Happened Next”  

---

## 4. Same Story – Different Headlines

Read the situation:

👉 A company reduces working hours slightly due to lower demand.

Now read the headlines:

1. “Company Cuts Jobs as Crisis Deepens”  
2. “Business Adjusts Working Hours Amid Market Changes”  
3. “Workers Affected by Sudden Company Decision”  

---

### Task

1. Which headline sounds:
- most dramatic?  
- most neutral?  
- most negative?  

2. Which one would you trust the most? Why?

---

## 5. Language Focus – Headlines

Look at how headlines differ from normal sentences:

- words are often omitted  
- verbs are simplified  
- strong words are preferred  

Example:

“Government to Introduce New Measures”  
(not: The government is going to introduce…)

---

### Task

Turn the headlines into full sentences.

1. “Prices Rise Again”  
2. “Government Faces Criticism”  
3. “New Law Causes Confusion”  
4. “Company Under Pressure”  

---

## 6. Interpretation Task

Read the headline:

👉 “Young People Losing Interest in Work”

---

### Task

Answer:

1. What assumptions does this headline suggest?  
2. What information is missing?  
3. What alternative explanations could there be?  

---

## 7. Critical Thinking – Choose the Best Headline

Situation:

👉 A student group organises a small protest about university fees.

Choose the most appropriate headline and justify:

- “Students Revolt Against System”  
- “Small Student Protest Takes Place”  
- “University Faces Major Crisis”  

---

## 8. Speaking (1:1)

Discuss:

1. Why do media exaggerate headlines?
2. Is it manipulation or just marketing?
3. Should headlines be more neutral?
4. How can readers avoid being misled?

👉 Focus:
- interpretation  
- evaluation  
- justification  

---

## 9. Real Task

Imagine you are writing a news headline for:

👉 A new app helps people save money.

Write:

1. one neutral headline  
2. one exaggerated headline  
3. one misleading headline  

---

## 10. Writing Task

Write **180–220 words**.

Topic:

**Are headlines misleading or necessary?**

Include:

- examples  
- reasons  
- impact on people  
- your opinion
