# Lesson 70 – Would You Want to Know the Truth?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:
- discuss truth, secrets and uncertainty naturally
- evaluate difficult moral choices
- express nuanced opinions about honesty and happiness
- reflect on whether ignorance can sometimes be beneficial

---

# 1. Thought Experiment

Read the statement.

## "Not knowing the truth can sometimes make people happier."

---

## Initial Reaction

Choose your position.

- Strongly agree
- Mostly agree
- Unsure
- Mostly disagree
- Strongly disagree

Explain your answer.

---

# 2. Four Difficult Situations

Imagine these situations.

## Situation A – The Future

Scientists invent a machine that can tell you exactly how many years you have left to live.

The prediction is always correct.

Would you want to know?

---

## Situation B – The Relationship

You discover that your partner was unfaithful twenty years ago.

Nobody knows except you.

Your relationship has been happy ever since.

Would you want to know the truth?

---

## Situation C – The Friend

You learn that a close friend secretly dislikes you and speaks badly about you behind your back.

Would you rather know or remain unaware?

---

## Situation D – The Lottery

You are guaranteed to live a happy life, but you will never know the complete truth about certain things.

Would you accept this?

---

# 3. First Conclusions

Discuss.

2. Is ignorance sometimes a blessing?
3. Are some secrets harmless?
4. Can truth destroy happiness?

# 4. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- uncomfortable truth – fact that is difficult to accept
- uncertainty – lack of complete knowledge
- ignorance – lack of awareness or information
- denial – refusal to accept reality
- secret – information intentionally hidden
- peace of mind – feeling of calm and security
- curiosity – desire to know something
- closure – emotional resolution
- reality – actual facts and circumstances
- self-deception – lying to yourself

# 7. Reading

Read.

Human beings naturally seek certainty and understanding. However, knowledge does not always bring comfort.

Many people avoid difficult truths because uncertainty can feel easier than confronting painful realities. Others argue that truth, even when unpleasant, allows people to make informed decisions and live authentically.

Psychologists suggest that people often balance two competing desires: the desire to know and the desire to protect themselves emotionally.

As a result, individuals sometimes avoid information that could threaten their happiness or identity.

Ultimately, deciding whether to seek the truth depends not only on facts, but also on values, personality and emotional readiness.
