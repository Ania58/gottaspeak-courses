# Lesson 107 – What Makes Someone Employable?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:

- discuss the qualities employers value most
- distinguish between technical and transferable skills
- evaluate the importance of continuous learning
- express informed opinions about career development

# 2. Five Recruitment Scenarios

Read each situation.

Your task:

- Decide who you would hire.
- Explain your reasoning.
- Identify which skills matter most.

---

## Situation 1 – Experience vs Potential

You need to hire a project coordinator.

### Candidate A

- ten years of experience
- resistant to change
- avoids learning new technologies

### Candidate B

- two years of experience
- highly adaptable
- eager to learn

---

## Situation 2 – Technical Expert

A software developer is exceptionally talented.

However:

- they struggle to communicate,
- dislike teamwork,
- regularly create conflicts within the team.

Should technical ability outweigh interpersonal skills?

---

## Situation 3 – Career Break

A candidate took a four-year career break to care for family members.

They have recently completed several online courses and professional certifications.

Would you consider hiring them?

---

## Situation 4 – AI Skills

Two applicants have similar qualifications.

One has experience using AI tools responsibly to improve productivity.

The other refuses to use AI because they believe it will replace human workers.

Who has the stronger long-term potential?

---

## Situation 5 – Perfect CV

A candidate has an outstanding CV.

During the interview, however, they cannot explain their previous projects clearly and appear unwilling to accept feedback.

How much should the interview influence the hiring decision?

---

## Discussion

1. Which candidate would you hire in each situation?
2. Can attitude compensate for limited experience?
3. Which soft skills are impossible to teach?
4. Are interviews reliable predictors of future performance?

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- employability – ability to obtain and maintain employment
- transferable skills – abilities that remain useful across different professions
- professional competence – combination of knowledge, skills and behaviour required for a job
- adaptability – willingness and ability to adjust to new situations
- continuous learning – ongoing process of developing new knowledge throughout one's career
- professional credibility – reputation for being competent and trustworthy
- problem-solving ability – capacity to analyse situations and develop effective solutions
- interpersonal effectiveness – ability to communicate and cooperate successfully with others
- growth potential – likelihood that someone will continue developing professionally
- career resilience – ability to adapt successfully to changes in the labour market

# 7. Reading

Read.

The modern labour market changes more rapidly than ever before. New technologies, automation and global competition continually reshape professional requirements, making lifelong learning increasingly important. As a result, employers often value adaptability and learning potential as highly as existing technical knowledge.

Transferable skills such as communication, collaboration, critical thinking and problem-solving allow employees to succeed across different industries. Unlike technical knowledge, which may become outdated, these abilities remain valuable even as professions evolve.

Research consistently suggests that organisations benefit from employees who demonstrate curiosity, resilience and a willingness to improve. Rather than searching for perfect candidates, many employers now prioritise individuals who show strong growth potential and can continue developing throughout their careers.
