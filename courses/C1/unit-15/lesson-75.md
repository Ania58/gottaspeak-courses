# Lesson 75 – Advice From Your 80-Year-Old Self

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:
- reflect on priorities and long-term happiness
- discuss regrets and life choices naturally
- express hopes and values
- imagine future perspectives and give advice

---

# 1. A Letter From the Future

Imagine you are eighty years old.

You are healthy, peaceful and looking back on your life.

One day, you are given the chance to send a letter to your present self.

You can only include a few pieces of advice.

---

## Questions

1. What would your 80-year-old self say?
2. What mistakes would they warn you about?
3. What would they tell you not to worry about?
4. What would they encourage you to do?

---

# 2. Four Things Older People Often Say

### "I wish I had worried less."

### "I wish I had spent more time with people I loved."

### "I wish I had taken more chances."

### "I wish I had enjoyed the present instead of waiting for the perfect moment."

---

## Discussion

1. Which statement resonates with you most?
2. Which one surprises you?
3. Why do people often realise these things late in life?
4. What do young people worry about unnecessarily?

---

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- look back on something – think about the past
- hindsight – understanding something better after it happened
- let something slip away – lose an opportunity by not acting
- live with regret – continue feeling sad about past decisions
- have no regrets – feel satisfied with your choices
- take a leap of faith – do something despite uncertainty
- make peace with something – accept something emotionally
- cherish something – appreciate something deeply
- lose sight of something – forget what is important
- put things into perspective – understand what truly matters

---

# 6. The Last Day Experiment

Imagine you discover that today is the last ordinary day of your life.

Tomorrow, everything will change forever.

You don't know how.

---

## Questions

2. What would you do?

# 7. Reading

Read.

Many older people report that their greatest regrets are not connected to money, possessions or status.

Instead, they often wish they had spent more time with loved ones, worried less about other people's opinions and taken more risks.

Psychologists suggest that with age, people naturally begin focusing less on achievement and more on meaning and emotional wellbeing.

As priorities shift, many discover that happiness comes not from extraordinary moments but from ordinary experiences shared with people they care about.

Perhaps wisdom is simply learning what deserves our attention and what does not.
