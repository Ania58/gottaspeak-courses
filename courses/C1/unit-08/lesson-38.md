## 5. Grammar – Anticipatory Framing & Evaluative Qualification

We analyse imagined futures and emotional anticipation:

- The mind may interpret uncertain outcomes as potential threats long before they occur  
- Anticipatory anxiety often develops not from reality itself, but from imagined possibilities  
- While anxiety can sometimes encourage preparation, it may simultaneously intensify helplessness  
- Fear of the future is not necessarily irrational, although it may become psychologically overwhelming

## 8. Reading

Read the text.

Anxiety is often associated with fear, yet the two experiences are not identical. Fear tends to emerge in response to immediate danger, whereas anxiety frequently concerns imagined or uncertain future events.

What makes anxiety particularly challenging is that the brain may respond to anticipated threats as though they were already real. Individuals therefore experience emotional and physiological reactions even when no immediate danger exists.

This process is closely connected to catastrophising — the tendency to imagine increasingly negative outcomes and interpret uncertainty pessimistically.

While moderate anxiety may encourage preparation and caution, chronic anxiety often reduces wellbeing and creates emotional exhaustion.
