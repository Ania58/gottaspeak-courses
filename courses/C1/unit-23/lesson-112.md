# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- active listening – consciously paying full attention to understand both the message and the speaker
- reflective response – reply showing that the listener has understood the speaker's message
- emotional validation – recognising and accepting another person's emotions without judging them
- conversational cue – verbal or non-verbal signal that guides the flow of a conversation
- attentive presence – giving someone complete attention without distraction
- reciprocal communication – balanced exchange in which both people listen and speak
- empathetic listening – listening with the intention of understanding another person's perspective and emotions
- verbal acknowledgement – short spoken response showing attention (e.g. "I see", "That makes sense")
- listening bias – tendency to interpret what someone says according to one's own expectations
- psychological safety – feeling able to speak honestly without fear of criticism or embarrassment

# 7. Reading

Read.

Listening is often treated as a passive activity, yet psychologists describe it as one of the most demanding communication skills. Effective listeners do far more than simply hear words. They interpret tone, notice body language, recognise emotions and continually check whether they have understood the speaker correctly.

Research shows that active listening strengthens trust, reduces conflict and improves collaboration. People who feel genuinely heard are generally more willing to cooperate, share information and consider alternative viewpoints. In contrast, conversations often become unproductive when participants focus primarily on preparing their own responses instead of understanding the other person.

Developing strong listening habits requires patience, curiosity and self-awareness. Asking thoughtful follow-up questions, avoiding unnecessary interruptions and acknowledging emotions all contribute to deeper and more meaningful communication.
