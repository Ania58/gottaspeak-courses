# Lesson 111 – Why Do We Misunderstand Each Other?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:

- discuss why misunderstandings occur in everyday communication
- analyse the role of assumptions and context
- identify common communication barriers
- express ideas clearly while avoiding ambiguity

# 2. Five Communication Scenarios

Read each situation.

Your task:

- Explain what caused the misunderstanding.
- Suggest how it could have been avoided.
- Discuss who was mainly responsible.

---

## Situation 1 – The Text Message

Emma sends a short message:

*"Fine."*

Her friend assumes she is angry.

Emma simply replied quickly because she was busy.

---

## Situation 2 – The Meeting

A manager tells an employee:

*"I'd like this finished soon."*

The employee believes this means sometime next week.

The manager expected it by the end of the day.

---

## Situation 3 – The International Team

During an online meeting, one colleague remains completely silent.

Some team members think they disagree.

Later they explain that, in their culture, interrupting others is considered disrespectful.

---

## Situation 4 – The Email

A customer receives a very brief email from customer support.

The message is technically correct but sounds cold and unfriendly.

The employee who wrote it intended to be efficient.

---

## Situation 5 – The Joke

Someone tells a sarcastic joke.

One person laughs.

Another feels deeply offended because they interpret the comment literally.

---

## Discussion

1. Which misunderstanding was easiest to avoid?
2. Does technology make misunderstandings more common?
3. How important is cultural context?
4. Should communicators always assume their message could be misunderstood?

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- misinterpretation – incorrect understanding of someone's words, actions or intentions
- implicit meaning – idea suggested without being stated directly
- ambiguity – quality of having more than one possible meaning
- context-dependent language – language whose meaning changes depending on the situation
- assumption – belief accepted without proof
- communication breakdown – failure in the exchange of information or understanding
- unintended message – meaning received by the listener that the speaker never intended
- perception gap – difference between how two people understand the same situation
- clarification – additional explanation intended to remove misunderstanding
- nuance – subtle difference in meaning or interpretation

# 7. Reading

Read.

People often assume that communication fails because someone chooses the wrong words. In reality, misunderstandings are frequently caused by assumptions, expectations and differences in interpretation rather than vocabulary itself.

Communication always depends on context. The same sentence may express encouragement, criticism, humour or sarcasm depending on tone of voice, facial expressions and the relationship between the speakers. Written communication removes many of these clues, making emails and text messages especially vulnerable to misunderstanding.

Successful communicators recognise that meaning is created jointly. Instead of assuming that their message has been understood exactly as intended, they encourage questions, provide clarification and remain open to different interpretations.
