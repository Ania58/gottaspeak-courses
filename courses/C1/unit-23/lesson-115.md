# Lesson 115 – What Makes Someone a Great Communicator?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:

- identify the characteristics of highly effective communicators
- analyse the relationship between communication and leadership
- evaluate the roles of authenticity, persuasion and credibility
- reflect on how communication influences personal and professional relationships

# 2. Five Communication Scenarios

Read each situation.

Your task:

- Explain why the communication succeeds or fails.
- Identify the communicator's strengths and weaknesses.
- Suggest possible improvements.

---

## Situation 1 – The Inspiring Leader

A team leader speaks confidently, explains ideas clearly and encourages everyone to contribute.

Employees leave meetings feeling motivated and respected.

---

## Situation 2 – The Brilliant Expert

A scientist possesses exceptional knowledge but struggles to explain complex ideas to non-specialists.

People admire their expertise but rarely understand their presentations.

---

## Situation 3 – The Persuasive Salesperson

A salesperson can convince almost anyone to buy a product.

However, they often exaggerate its advantages.

Customers eventually stop trusting them.

---

## Situation 4 – The Quiet Colleague

One employee rarely speaks during meetings.

When they do contribute, everyone listens carefully because their comments are thoughtful and well supported.

---

## Situation 5 – The Social Media Influencer

An online creator has millions of followers.

They communicate confidently and attract attention but frequently spread inaccurate information.

Many people still trust them.

---

## Discussion

1. Which communicator would you most like to work with?
2. Is confidence more important than expertise?
3. Can someone be persuasive without being trustworthy?
4. Which quality matters most: clarity, empathy or credibility?

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- persuasive communication – ability to influence others through convincing arguments and effective delivery
- authenticity – quality of being genuine and true to one's values
- rhetorical skill – ability to communicate ideas in a compelling and well-structured way
- credibility – quality of being trustworthy and believable
- rapport – positive relationship based on mutual understanding and trust
- interpersonal influence – ability to affect other people's attitudes or behaviour
- emotional intelligence – ability to recognise and manage one's own emotions and those of others
- audience engagement – ability to maintain listeners' attention and involvement
- articulate speaker – person who expresses ideas clearly and effectively
- communication style – characteristic way in which someone communicates

# 7. Reading

Read.

Great communicators are often associated with confidence and charisma, yet research suggests that these qualities alone rarely create lasting influence. People are far more likely to trust speakers who demonstrate authenticity, consistency and genuine interest in others.

Effective communication involves much more than speaking clearly. Skilled communicators adapt their language to their audience, listen carefully, ask thoughtful questions and remain aware of emotional responses. They understand that persuasion is built not only through logical arguments but also through credibility and trust.

Perhaps the most important quality of outstanding communicators is their ability to make others feel respected and understood. Whether leading organisations, teaching students or resolving conflicts, successful communicators recognise that meaningful dialogue depends on mutual understanding rather than simply delivering information.
