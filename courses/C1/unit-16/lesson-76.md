# Lesson 76 – Can Money Buy Happiness?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:
- discuss money and happiness naturally
- express opinions about wealth and life satisfaction
- compare material success and emotional wellbeing
- analyse what contributes to a good life

---

# 1. Debate Starter

## "Money can't buy happiness."

---

## Initial Reaction

- Strongly agree
- Mostly agree
- Unsure
- Mostly disagree
- Strongly disagree

# 2. Three Stories

## Emma

Emma earns a modest salary. She enjoys her work, has close friends and spends most weekends with her family.

She says:

> "I don't have everything I want, but I have everything I need."

---

## Ryan

Ryan became wealthy before the age of forty.

He owns expensive cars and travels frequently, but admits that he often feels lonely and stressed.

---

## Mia

Mia struggled financially for years.

After getting a better job, she noticed that her quality of life improved dramatically.

She says:

> "Money didn't make me happy, but it removed many worries."

---

## Discussion

1. Which person seems happiest?
2. Can money solve problems?
3. Is there a difference between comfort and happiness?
4. Does poverty make happiness more difficult?

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- financial security – having enough money to live comfortably without constant worry
- make ends meet – have just enough money to cover basic expenses
- cost of living – amount of money needed for everyday life
- peace of mind – feeling of calm because you are free from worries
- lifestyle inflation – tendency to spend more as income increases
- live beyond your means – spend more money than you can afford
- source of fulfilment – thing that gives life meaning and satisfaction
- material possessions – physical things that people own
- sense of purpose – feeling that life has meaning
- quality of life – overall level of comfort and wellbeing

# 7. Reading

Read.

Many people associate happiness with wealth, fame and material success. However, research consistently shows that once people's basic needs are satisfied, additional income has a surprisingly limited effect on long-term happiness.

Money can reduce stress, improve health and provide opportunities. Yet relationships, purpose and meaningful experiences often have a greater impact on wellbeing.

Psychologists suggest that people quickly adapt to improvements in lifestyle. What once seemed luxurious gradually becomes normal, leading people to pursue even more.

As a result, happiness may depend less on how much money people have and more on how they choose to live.
