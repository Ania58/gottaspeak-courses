# Lesson 79 – Saving or Enjoying Life?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:
- discuss saving and spending habits naturally
- evaluate different approaches to money
- talk about risk, security and delayed gratification
- express opinions about balancing the present and the future

---

# 1. Three People, Three Strategies

Read the cases.

Your task:

- Decide who has the healthiest attitude.
- Identify the advantages and disadvantages of each approach.
- Explain which lifestyle you would prefer.

---

## Case A – Emma

Emma believes life is short.

She spends most of her money on travel, concerts and experiences.

She has wonderful memories but very little savings.

She says:

> "You can't take money to the grave."

---

## Case B – David

David saves almost everything.

He rarely goes on holiday, avoids restaurants and constantly worries about money.

He dreams of retiring early but often feels he is missing out on life.

---

## Case C – Sophia

Sophia saves regularly but also allows herself to enjoy life.

She plans for the future while making time for travel and hobbies.

She says:

> "Tomorrow matters, but so does today."

---

## Discussion

1. Which person seems happiest?
2. Which approach would you choose?
3. Can people become obsessed with saving?
4. Can living only for today be dangerous?

# 2. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- delayed gratification – ability to wait for future rewards
- rainy day fund – money saved for emergencies
- live in the moment – focus on enjoying the present
- miss out on something – fail to experience something enjoyable
- financial cushion – extra savings that provide security
- short-term pleasure – enjoyment that lasts briefly
- long-term security – stability and protection in the future
- sacrifice today for tomorrow – give up current pleasures for future benefits
- strike a balance – find a healthy middle ground
- peace of mind – freedom from worry

# 6. Reading

Read.

People often struggle to balance present happiness with future security.

Some focus so heavily on saving that they postpone enjoyment indefinitely. Others spend freely and ignore long-term consequences.

Psychologists describe this conflict as a tension between immediate gratification and delayed gratification.

Neither extreme appears to bring lasting happiness. Research suggests that people who combine responsible planning with meaningful experiences often report greater life satisfaction.

Perhaps money is not simply something to save or spend, but a tool that should support the life people want to build.
