# Lesson 77 – Are We Too Materialistic?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:
- discuss consumerism and spending habits naturally
- compare different attitudes towards possessions
- analyse social pressure and lifestyle choices
- express opinions about minimalism and materialism

---

# 1. The Investigation

Three people have very different lifestyles.

Your task:

- Decide who seems happiest.
- Identify the advantages and disadvantages of each lifestyle.
- Discuss whether possessions really improve life.

---

## Case A – Olivia

Olivia owns very little.

She lives in a small apartment, rarely buys new clothes and prefers experiences over things.

She says:

> "Less stuff means less stress."

---

## Case B – Ethan

Ethan enjoys luxury.

He buys expensive watches, drives a sports car and likes showing his success.

He says:

> "I work hard, so why shouldn't I enjoy it?"

---

## Case C – Chloe

Chloe often buys things impulsively.

Packages arrive almost every day, but she quickly loses interest in what she buys.

She frequently complains that she never has enough money.

---

## Discussion

1. Which person seems happiest?
2. Which lifestyle would you prefer?
3. Can possessions become a source of stress?
4. Why do people buy things they don't need?

# 2. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- consumerism – society's tendency to buy and consume excessively
- impulse buying – purchasing something without planning
- instant gratification – immediate pleasure without waiting
- social pressure – influence from other people or society
- keep up with the Joneses – try to match other people's lifestyle and possessions
- clutter – too many unnecessary things
- retail therapy – shopping to improve your mood
- buyer's remorse – regret after buying something
- status symbol – possession that represents wealth or success
- minimalist lifestyle – life focused on owning fewer things

# 6. Reading

Read.

Modern society encourages people to consume constantly. Advertising, social media and peer pressure often create the impression that happiness depends on owning more.

However, psychologists have found that the excitement of buying new things usually fades quickly. People adapt to possessions and soon desire something else.

Experiences, meaningful relationships and a sense of purpose often provide more lasting satisfaction than material goods.

This does not mean that possessions are unimportant, but many people eventually discover that owning more does not necessarily mean living better.
