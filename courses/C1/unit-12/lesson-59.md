# Lesson 59 – Minimalism and Owning Less

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:
- discuss consumerism and modern lifestyles naturally
- talk about possessions, spending and priorities
- express opinions about simplicity and materialism
- reflect on what truly adds value to life

---

# 1. Case Study

Read the situation.

A few years ago, Ethan and Mia lived what many people would consider a successful life.

They had well-paid jobs, a large house and constantly bought new things. Every year they upgraded their phones, changed their furniture and spent a lot of money on clothes and gadgets.

Despite this, they often felt stressed about money and complained that they never had enough time.

One day, after moving to another city, they decided to simplify their lives.

They sold many of their possessions, stopped buying things they didn't really need and focused more on experiences than on material goods.

Surprisingly, they discovered that owning less gave them a greater sense of freedom.

Their friends found the change strange. Some admired them, while others thought they had become boring or overly extreme.

# 3. Language From the Situation

Work with these expressions.

- consumerism – society's tendency to buy and consume more and more
- material possessions – physical things people own
- clutter – unnecessary things creating disorder
- financial pressure – stress caused by money concerns

- lifestyle choice – conscious decision about how to live
- unnecessary expense – money spent on things that are not essential
- fulfilment – deep satisfaction and meaning
- freedom – ability to live according to your priorities
- priorities – things that matter most

# 7. Reading

Read.

Modern society often encourages people to believe that happiness comes from acquiring more.

Advertising, social media and consumer culture constantly expose people to new desires and expectations.

However, studies suggest that once basic needs are met, additional possessions provide diminishing returns. People quickly adapt to new purchases and often return to their previous level of happiness.

As a result, some individuals choose to focus less on ownership and more on experiences, relationships and freedom.

Minimalism does not necessarily mean living with almost nothing. Rather, it means becoming more intentional about what deserves space, time and attention.
