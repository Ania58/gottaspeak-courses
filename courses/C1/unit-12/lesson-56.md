# Lesson 56 – Why Are We Never Satisfied?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:
- discuss happiness and satisfaction naturally
- analyse the role of comparison and expectations
- talk about gratitude and contentment
- express nuanced opinions about success and happiness

---

# 1. The Investigation

Three people seem unhappy, but for different reasons.

Your task:

- Identify the problem.
- Decide whether the problem comes from circumstances or mindset.
- Suggest a solution.

---

## Case A – Olivia

Olivia finally bought her dream house after years of saving.

For the first few months she was thrilled.

But now she barely notices it.

Instead, she spends hours looking at social media and comparing herself with people who have bigger homes, better holidays and more luxurious lifestyles.

She constantly feels as though she is falling behind.

---

## Case B – Nathan

Nathan has spent years chasing promotions and salary increases.

Every time he reaches a goal, he immediately starts focusing on the next one.

He tells himself that he'll finally relax and enjoy life once he reaches a certain level of success.

But somehow, that moment never arrives.

---

## Case C – Sophia

Sophia lives a fairly simple life.

She earns an average salary, spends time with friends and family and enjoys ordinary routines.

However, people around her often ask why she isn't more ambitious.

Sometimes she wonders whether she should want more.

# 3. Language From the Cases

Work with these expressions.

- contentment – a feeling of peaceful satisfaction
- expectations – beliefs about what should happen
- comparison trap – habit of constantly comparing yourself to others
- gratitude – appreciation for what you already have
- fulfilment – deep satisfaction and meaning
- dissatisfaction – feeling that something is missing
- ambition – strong desire to achieve something
- material possessions – physical things people own
- lifestyle inflation – spending more as income increases
- hedonic treadmill – tendency to quickly adapt to improvements and want more

# 7. Reading

Read.

Human beings adapt surprisingly quickly to positive changes.

Research suggests that after achieving major goals, buying expensive possessions or receiving promotions, people often return to their previous level of happiness sooner than expected.

This tendency is sometimes called the "hedonic treadmill".

That does not mean goals and ambition are meaningless. Rather, it suggests that lasting satisfaction may depend less on constant achievement and more on relationships, gratitude and a sense of purpose.

The challenge is finding a balance between striving for improvement and appreciating what already exists.
