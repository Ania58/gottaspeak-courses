# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- conspiracy theory – explanation claiming that powerful groups secretly control important events without convincing evidence
- speculation – idea based on incomplete information rather than verified facts
- evidence – information supporting or disproving a claim
- coincidence – events occurring together without a meaningful causal connection
- logical fallacy – mistake in reasoning that weakens an argument
- burden of proof – responsibility to provide evidence for a claim
- extraordinary claim – assertion that challenges accepted knowledge and therefore requires particularly strong evidence
- critical scepticism – willingness to question claims while remaining open to evidence
- correlation – statistical relationship between two events that does not necessarily imply causation
- causation – direct relationship in which one event produces another

# 7. Reading

Read.

Conspiracy theories often emerge during periods of uncertainty, fear or rapid social change. When important events lack simple explanations, many people naturally search for patterns that create a sense of order. Psychological research suggests that humans are especially skilled at recognising patterns, even when those patterns are purely coincidental.

This tendency may lead individuals to confuse correlation with causation. Two events occurring at the same time do not necessarily mean that one caused the other. Likewise, extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence. Suspicion alone cannot replace carefully collected facts, independent verification or logical reasoning.

Critical thinking does not require blind trust in authority. Instead, it encourages people to evaluate evidence fairly, compare multiple explanations and remain willing to change their opinions when convincing evidence becomes available.
