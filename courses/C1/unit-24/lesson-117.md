# Lesson 117 – Why Do Smart People Believe False Information?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:

- explain why intelligent people can believe false information
- distinguish between misinformation and disinformation
- analyse the role of cognitive biases in evaluating evidence
- discuss strategies for verifying information critically

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- misinformation – false or inaccurate information shared without the intention to deceive
- disinformation – deliberately false information spread to mislead people
- confirmation bias – tendency to favour information that supports existing beliefs
- echo chamber – environment in which people mainly encounter opinions similar to their own
- cognitive dissonance – psychological discomfort experienced when evidence conflicts with existing beliefs
- source credibility – degree to which a source is trustworthy and reliable
- media literacy – ability to access, evaluate and critically analyse media content
- fact-checking – process of verifying whether information is accurate
- misleading claim – statement that creates a false impression even if not entirely false
- information overload – situation in which the amount of available information makes good decision-making more difficult

# 7. Reading

Read.

Contrary to popular belief, intelligence alone does not protect people from believing false information. In fact, highly educated individuals may sometimes become especially confident in their own judgement, making them less likely to question information that supports their existing beliefs.

Psychologists explain this partly through confirmation bias. Rather than evaluating all available evidence objectively, people naturally pay greater attention to information that confirms what they already believe while dismissing contradictory evidence. Social media algorithms may reinforce this tendency by repeatedly showing users similar opinions, creating echo chambers where alternative perspectives become increasingly rare.

For this reason, media literacy has become an essential skill. Verifying sources, comparing independent reports and remaining willing to revise one's opinions when new evidence appears are all fundamental aspects of critical thinking in the digital age.
