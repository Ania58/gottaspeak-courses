## 🎯 Lesson Objective

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- intellectual humility – willingness to recognise the limits of one's own knowledge and revise one's beliefs when convincing evidence emerges
- epistemic vigilance – habit of critically evaluating information, sources and arguments before accepting them as reliable
- motivated reasoning – tendency to interpret evidence in ways that support preferred conclusions rather than objective truth
- metacognition – awareness and evaluation of one's own thinking processes and decision-making strategies
- fallacious reasoning – pattern of argument containing logical errors that undermine the validity of a conclusion
- intellectual integrity – commitment to consistency, honesty and fairness when evaluating evidence and defending opinions
- epistemic uncertainty – recognition that available evidence may be incomplete or insufficient for firm conclusions
- inferential reasoning – process of reaching conclusions through logical interpretation of available evidence rather than direct observation
- cognitive blind spot – inability to recognise one's own cognitive biases while identifying them easily in others
- reasoned judgement – conclusion reached through careful analysis of evidence, competing explanations and logical argumentation

# 7. Reading

Read.

Critical thinking is often misunderstood as simply questioning everything. In reality, it is a disciplined approach to evaluating information, arguments and evidence with intellectual honesty. Critical thinkers neither accept claims blindly nor reject them automatically. Instead, they proportion their confidence to the quality of the available evidence.

An essential aspect of critical thinking is intellectual humility—the recognition that our beliefs may be incomplete or mistaken. This does not imply indecisiveness. Rather, it reflects a willingness to revise conclusions when stronger evidence emerges. Equally important is metacognition: the ability to reflect on how our own biases, assumptions and emotions influence our reasoning.

Ultimately, critical thinking is less about always being right and more about developing reliable methods for arriving at well-supported conclusions. It requires curiosity, intellectual integrity and the courage to admit uncertainty when certainty is not justified.
