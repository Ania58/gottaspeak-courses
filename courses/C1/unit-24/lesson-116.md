# Lesson 116 – Can You Trust Your First Impression?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:

- discuss how first impressions are formed
- analyse the influence of stereotypes and cognitive biases
- evaluate the reliability of intuition
- reflect on situations where first impressions were accurate or misleading

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- first impression – initial opinion formed when meeting someone or encountering something for the first time
- snap judgment – opinion formed very quickly without careful consideration
- confirmation bias – tendency to notice information that supports existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence
- stereotype – oversimplified belief about a particular group of people
- cognitive bias – systematic error in thinking that influences judgement and decision-making
- prejudice – opinion formed without sufficient knowledge or experience
- intuition – ability to understand or judge something without conscious reasoning
- objectivity – ability to evaluate something fairly and without personal bias
- subconscious assumption – belief formed automatically without conscious awareness
- halo effect – cognitive bias in which one positive characteristic influences overall judgement of a person

# 7. Reading

Read.

Psychologists have long known that first impressions form remarkably quickly, often within just a few seconds of meeting someone. These impressions are influenced by appearance, facial expressions, tone of voice, body language and countless subconscious assumptions. Although rapid judgements may have helped humans identify potential threats throughout evolution, they are far from perfect.

One reason first impressions can be unreliable is the influence of cognitive biases. Confirmation bias encourages people to search for evidence that supports their initial opinion while ignoring contradictory information. Similarly, the halo effect allows one positive characteristic, such as confidence or physical attractiveness, to shape a much broader judgement about a person's intelligence, honesty or competence.

For this reason, critical thinkers recognise that first impressions should be treated as hypotheses rather than conclusions. Remaining curious, asking questions and allowing new evidence to influence our opinions helps reduce unfair judgements and leads to more accurate understanding of others.
