# Lesson 101 – Why Do Smart People Make Bad Decisions?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:

- discuss why intelligent people sometimes make poor decisions
- analyse common cognitive biases
- evaluate flawed reasoning in real-life situations
- justify better decision-making strategies

---

## Case 1 – The Confident Investor

A successful engineer decides to invest almost all of their savings in a single company because they are convinced they understand the market better than financial experts.

---

A doctor immediately diagnoses a patient with a common illness because the symptoms appear familiar.

Several days later, tests reveal a much rarer condition that required different treatment.

---

## Case 3 – The Group Project

A project team agrees with the manager's proposal even though several members privately believe it will fail.

Nobody wants to challenge the group's opinion.

---

## Case 4 – The Entrepreneur

---

## Case 5 – The Student

Application deadlines are missed.

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- overconfidence bias – tendency to overestimate one's own knowledge or abilities
- sunk cost fallacy – continuing something because of previous investments rather than future value
- groupthink – tendency for group members to prioritise agreement over critical evaluation
- confirmation bias – tendency to seek information that supports existing beliefs
- decision paralysis – inability to make a decision because of too many alternatives or excessive analysis
- flawed reasoning – logical process that leads to an incorrect conclusion
- risk assessment – careful evaluation of possible dangers and benefits before acting
- judgement lapse – temporary failure to make a sensible decision
- cognitive distortion – systematic error in thinking that affects judgement
- analytical thinking – careful evaluation of evidence before reaching a conclusion

# 7. Reading

Read.

People often assume that intelligence naturally leads to better decisions. However, research in psychology suggests that decision-making depends on much more than knowledge alone. Even highly intelligent individuals rely on mental shortcuts and cognitive biases, particularly when making decisions under pressure or uncertainty.

One common example is overconfidence bias, where people place excessive trust in their own judgement while underestimating possible risks. Another is confirmation bias, which encourages individuals to search for evidence that supports their existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory information.

Effective decision-makers recognise these limitations. Rather than assuming they are always correct, they actively question their assumptions, seek alternative perspectives and remain willing to change their minds when presented with new evidence.
