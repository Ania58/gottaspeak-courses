# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- choice overload – situation in which too many options make decision-making more difficult
- decision fatigue – decline in decision quality after making many choices
- satisficer – person who chooses an option that is good enough rather than searching endlessly for the best one
- maximiser – person who continually searches for the optimal choice
- anticipated regret – expectation of future disappointment before making a decision
- cognitive effort – mental energy required to process information
- trade-off analysis – comparison of advantages and disadvantages before choosing
- subjective satisfaction – personal feeling of contentment after making a decision
- opportunity cost – benefit lost by rejecting alternative options
- decision efficiency – ability to make effective decisions without unnecessary delay

# 7. Reading

Read.

Modern societies offer people more choices than ever before. Consumers can compare thousands of products, careers, universities and even potential partners within minutes. While this freedom appears beneficial, psychologists have discovered that excessive choice often produces unexpected consequences.

Choice overload increases cognitive effort and makes decisions feel more demanding. Instead of feeling satisfied, people may continue wondering whether another option would have produced a better outcome. This tendency is particularly common among maximisers, who constantly search for the perfect solution.

Research suggests that satisficers—people who choose an option that meets their needs without endlessly comparing alternatives—often report greater happiness. Their decisions may not always be objectively optimal, but they are more likely to experience satisfaction because they spend less time second-guessing themselves.
