# Lesson 103 – Why Do We Regret Some Decisions?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:

- discuss the psychology of regret
- analyse why some decisions continue to affect us for years
- distinguish between action regret and inaction regret
- reflect on the role of hindsight in personal growth

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- hindsight bias – tendency to believe that past events were more predictable than they actually were
- counterfactual thinking – imagining alternative outcomes by asking "what if?"
- opportunity regret – disappointment caused by a missed chance
- emotional resilience – ability to recover emotionally after setbacks or disappointment
- irreversible decision – choice that cannot easily be changed or undone
- psychological closure – emotional acceptance that allows someone to move forward
- self-forgiveness – process of accepting one's mistakes without continual self-blame
- life trajectory – long-term direction that a person's life follows
- retrospective evaluation – judging past decisions using information unavailable at the time
- emotional burden – lasting psychological weight caused by unresolved experiences

# 7. Reading

Read.

Regret is one of the most complex human emotions because it requires people to compare reality with imagined alternatives. Psychologists describe this process as counterfactual thinking, in which individuals mentally construct different versions of the past and wonder how life might have unfolded under different circumstances.

Interestingly, research suggests that short-term regrets often concern actions people wish they had not taken. Over longer periods, however, people more commonly regret opportunities they failed to pursue. Missed experiences continue to generate uncertainty because their outcomes remain permanently unknown.

Healthy reflection differs from unhealthy rumination. Reflection encourages learning and personal growth, whereas rumination repeatedly revisits the same events without producing new understanding. Developing self-forgiveness and psychological closure allows people to acknowledge past mistakes without allowing them to dominate the future.
