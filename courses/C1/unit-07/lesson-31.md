## 4. Grammar – Hedging & Epistemic Caution 

We avoid sounding overly absolute:

- There appears to be evidence suggesting that emotional reactions strongly influence belief formation  
- It could reasonably be argued that misinformation spreads more effectively when it triggers fear or anger  
- The claim may be somewhat misleading when examined more critically  
- What initially appears convincing may not necessarily be reliable

## 5. Grammar – Speculative Evaluation 

We analyse causes and interpretations carefully:

- The phenomenon is likely driven by emotional rather than rational responses  
- This may partly explain why misinformation spreads so effectively online  
- People may be more willing to accept information that reinforces existing beliefs  
- What is presented as objective truth may in fact reflect ideological influence

## 8. Reading

Read the text.

Misinformation spreads rapidly in modern digital environments, partly because people often respond emotionally before evaluating information critically. Content that triggers fear, anger or outrage tends to attract attention more effectively than neutral information.

Another important factor is confirmation bias. Individuals are generally more likely to accept information that supports their existing beliefs while dismissing evidence that challenges them.

Social media algorithms may intensify this tendency by exposing users primarily to content similar to what they already engage with. As a result, people can gradually become trapped in informational bubbles where certain narratives are continuously reinforced.

What makes this phenomenon particularly concerning is that emotional conviction may eventually feel more persuasive than factual accuracy.
