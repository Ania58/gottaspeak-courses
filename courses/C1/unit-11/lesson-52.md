# Lesson 52 – Good Decisions, Bad Decisions

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:
- discuss decision-making processes naturally
- evaluate choices and consequences
- talk about regret, uncertainty and hindsight
- express balanced opinions about difficult decisions

---

# 1. The Investigation

Today you are the investigator.

You will examine three situations and decide:

- Who made the best decision?
- Who made the worst decision?
- Could the outcomes have been predicted?
- Would you have acted differently?

---

# Case A – The Safe Choice

Emma was offered two jobs.

The first offered excellent pay and long-term security, but the work itself did not interest her.

The second offered lower pay and less stability, but the role matched her interests perfectly.

After weeks of thinking, she accepted the safer option.

Three years later, she is financially comfortable but often wonders whether she made the right choice.

---

# Case B – The Impulsive Choice

Marcus saw an advertisement for a business opportunity.

Without doing much research, he invested most of his savings.

The company collapsed within six months.

Marcus lost a significant amount of money and later admitted he had ignored several warning signs because he was excited by the potential rewards.

---

# Case C – The Delayed Choice

Sophie wanted to move abroad.

She spent years researching different countries, comparing costs and reading advice online.

Every time she felt ready, she found another reason to wait.

Ten years later, she still had not moved and often thought about what might have happened if she had gone.

---

# 3. Language From the Cases

Work with these expressions.

- weigh up options – carefully consider alternatives
- judgement call – decision based on experience rather than certainty
- hindsight – understanding a situation better after it has happened
- uncertainty – lack of certainty about outcomes
- calculated risk – risk taken after careful consideration
- impulsive decision – decision made quickly without enough thought
- missed opportunity – chance that was not taken
- long-term consequences – effects that appear later
- second-guess yourself – repeatedly question your decisions
- trade-off – accepting one advantage while losing another

# 7. Reading

Read.

Many people believe good decisions always produce good outcomes. In reality, life is often more complicated.

A sensible decision can sometimes lead to disappointing results, while a reckless decision may occasionally succeed.

For this reason, experts often recommend evaluating decisions based on the information available at the time rather than on the final outcome.

The quality of a decision depends not only on what happened afterwards, but also on how carefully the decision was made.
