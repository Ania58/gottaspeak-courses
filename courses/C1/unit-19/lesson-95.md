# Lesson 95 – What Would You Sacrifice?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:

- discuss ethical trade-offs confidently
- evaluate competing moral values
- defend difficult decisions with well-developed arguments
- reflect on personal priorities and responsibility

---

# 1. Opening Question

Read the statement.

## "Every important decision requires giving something up."

Do you agree?

---

## Discussion

1. What is the hardest decision you've ever made?
2. Is sacrificing something always necessary?
3. Which is more important: loyalty, honesty or fairness?
4. Can every decision be morally justified?

# 2. Five Moral Choices

Read the situations.

There is **no perfect solution**.

Your task:

- Choose what you would do.
- Explain why.
- Discuss what you would lose by making that choice.

---

## Situation 1 – The Confidential File

You discover confidential documents proving that your company has been breaking environmental regulations.

Reporting the information would protect thousands of people.

However, it would also cause hundreds of your colleagues to lose their jobs.

---

## Situation 2 – The Best Friend

Your closest friend causes a serious car accident.

Nobody else witnessed it.

The police ask whether you saw what happened.

---

## Situation 3 – The Scholarship

A university accidentally offers you a scholarship that should have gone to another student.

Nobody notices the mistake.

Accepting it would completely change your future.

---

## Situation 4 – The Family Business

Your family owns a successful business.

You discover that some products are being advertised dishonestly.

Exposing the truth would destroy the company's reputation and threaten your family's financial security.

---

## Situation 5 – The Hospital

Only one intensive care bed remains.

Two patients urgently need it.

One is a 25-year-old with an excellent chance of recovery.

The other is an 80-year-old who has contributed enormously to society throughout their life.

---

## Discussion

1. Which decision was the most difficult?
2. Which values conflicted in each situation?
3. Did any answer make you uncomfortable?
4. Can there ever be a completely fair decision?

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- competing moral claims – ethical obligations that cannot all be satisfied simultaneously
- value hierarchy – ranking of personal principles according to importance
- distributive justice – fairness in the allocation of limited resources
- ethical proportionality – judging whether the benefits of an action justify its costs
- moral legitimacy – ethical acceptability of a decision or action
- competing interests – conflicting needs or priorities of different individuals or groups
- irreversible consequence – outcome that cannot be undone
- ethical consistency – applying the same moral standards in similar situations
- conflict of conscience – internal struggle between incompatible moral beliefs
- zero-sum dilemma – situation in which one person's gain necessarily requires another person's loss

# 7. Reading

Read.

Many ethical dilemmas arise because important moral values come into conflict. Loyalty may oppose honesty, compassion may conflict with fairness, and protecting one individual may require sacrificing the interests of many others.

Philosophers disagree about whether there is a universal method for resolving such conflicts. Some argue that decisions should maximise overall wellbeing, while others insist that certain rights should never be violated, regardless of the outcome.

In practice, people rarely rely on a single ethical principle. Instead, they weigh competing interests, anticipate possible consequences and reflect on their own moral values before making a judgement.

This complexity explains why reasonable people often reach very different conclusions when facing the same dilemma.
