# Lesson 91 – Would You Tell the Truth?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:

- discuss ethical dilemmas confidently
- evaluate difficult moral decisions
- justify opinions using nuanced arguments
- analyse the consequences of honesty and deception

---

# 1. The Opening Question

Read the statement.

## "Honesty is always the best policy."

Do you agree?

---

## Discussion

1. Is it ever acceptable to lie?
2. Does the intention behind a lie matter?
3. Can complete honesty damage relationships?
4. Would you rather hear a painful truth or a comforting lie?

---

# 2. Five Ethical Dilemmas

Read each situation.

Your task:

- Decide what you would do.
- Explain your reasoning.
- Predict the possible consequences.

---

## Situation 1 – The Wallet

You find a wallet containing €800 in cash.

There is no identification inside.

Nobody saw you pick it up.

What would you do?

---

## Situation 2 – The Job Interview

Your friend asks whether you think they performed well in an interview.

You know they performed poorly.

Would you tell the truth?

---

## Situation 3 – The Medical Diagnosis

A close relative asks whether the doctor seemed optimistic after a difficult appointment.

You know the diagnosis is serious, but the doctor wanted to explain everything personally.

Do you tell them what you know?

---

## Situation 4 – The Exam

You notice a classmate cheating during an important exam.

If you report them, they will probably fail.

If you stay silent, nobody will ever know.

---

## Situation 5 – The Secret

Your best friend tells you something deeply personal and asks you never to reveal it.

Later you realise that keeping the secret could seriously harm someone else.

What would you do?

---

## Discussion

1. Which dilemma was the most difficult?
2. Which decision depends most on the circumstances?
3. Are some lies morally preferable to others?

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- moral integrity – commitment to acting according to ethical principles
- conflicting obligations – responsibilities that cannot all be fulfilled at the same time
- ethical justification – moral reasoning used to defend a decision
- unintended repercussions – unexpected negative consequences of an action
- moral accountability – responsibility for the ethical consequences of one's actions
- deliberate deception – intentionally causing someone to believe something false
- breach of trust – action that destroys confidence in someone
- moral compromise – accepting behaviour that conflicts with one's principles
- duty of care – responsibility to protect the wellbeing of others
- conscientious objection – refusal to do something because of deeply held moral beliefs

# 7. Reading

Read.

Ethical dilemmas arise when people must choose between competing moral principles rather than between right and wrong. In such situations, every available option carries some negative consequence.

Philosophers have long debated whether morality should be judged according to intentions or outcomes. Some argue that certain actions, such as lying or breaking promises, remain morally wrong regardless of the consequences. Others believe that decisions should be evaluated according to the amount of harm or benefit they produce.

In everyday life, ethical decisions are rarely clear-cut. Personal values, cultural expectations and emotional factors often influence the choices people make, even when they believe they are acting rationally.
