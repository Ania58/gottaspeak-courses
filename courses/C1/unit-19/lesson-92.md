# Lesson 92 – Is It Ever Right to Break the Rules?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:

- discuss the purpose of rules and laws
- evaluate situations in which breaking rules may be justified
- defend complex ethical viewpoints
- distinguish between legal and moral responsibility

---

# 1. Debate Starter

Read the statement.

## "Rules exist for a reason, so they should never be broken."

Do you agree?

---

## Discussion

1. Why do societies create rules?
2. Are all rules fair?
3. Should people always obey the law?
4. Is breaking a rule always unethical?

Read the situations.

- Decide whether breaking the rule is justified.
- Explain your reasoning.
- Consider both legal and moral consequences.

---

## Situation 1 – The Speed Limit

A father is driving his seriously injured child to the nearest hospital.

The road is empty.

He exceeds the speed limit.

---

## Situation 2 – The Museum

A visitor ignores the "Do Not Touch" sign to stop an expensive sculpture from falling.

---

## Situation 3 – The Whistleblower

An employee secretly shares confidential company documents because they reveal serious environmental damage that the company has hidden for years.

---

## Situation 4 – The Curfew

A teenager leaves home after midnight despite a strict family rule because a close friend calls asking for urgent help.

---

## Situation 5 – The Exam

A student notices that an invigilator has made a serious mistake which would cause many students to fail unfairly.

The student speaks during the exam, even though talking is strictly forbidden.

---

## Discussion

1. Which decision was the easiest to justify?
2. Which situation was the most controversial?
3. Should consequences outweigh the rules themselves?

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- civil disobedience – deliberate violation of a law to protest perceived injustice
- proportional response – reaction that is appropriate to the seriousness of a situation
- legitimate authority – power that is recognised as lawful or justified
- mitigating circumstances – factors that reduce responsibility for an action
- ethical accountability – responsibility for the moral consequences of one's behaviour
- abuse of authority – improper use of power for personal or institutional benefit
- legal compliance – acting in accordance with the law
- moral imperative – action considered ethically necessary regardless of personal cost
- institutional trust – confidence in organisations and public institutions
- rule of law – principle that everyone is equally subject to the law

# 7. Reading

Read.

Most societies depend on rules to maintain order and protect individual rights. Without shared expectations, cooperation would become difficult and legal systems could not function effectively.

However, history contains many examples of individuals who deliberately broke the law in order to challenge injustice. Acts of civil disobedience have contributed to significant social changes, including movements for civil rights, democracy and equality.

This creates a difficult ethical question. If a law is unjust, should people obey it simply because it is legal? Some philosophers argue that respect for the rule of law must always come first. Others believe that moral responsibility may occasionally require individuals to resist laws that violate fundamental human rights.
