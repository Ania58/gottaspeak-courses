# Lesson 94 – Should Intentions Matter More Than Outcomes?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:

- distinguish between intentions and consequences
- evaluate moral responsibility in complex situations
- justify ethical decisions using sophisticated arguments
- discuss accountability and unintended outcomes

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- consequentialist reasoning – judging actions primarily by their outcomes
- deontological principle – belief that some actions are right or wrong regardless of consequences
- moral culpability – degree of responsibility for wrongdoing
- foreseeable consequence – result that could reasonably have been predicted
- ethical trade-off – situation requiring the sacrifice of one moral value to protect another
- good-faith decision – action taken with honest and sincere intentions
- unintended harm – damage caused without the intention to do so
- moral justification – ethical reasoning used to defend an action
- proportional responsibility – responsibility that reflects a person's actual role in an outcome
- ethical ambiguity – situation in which there is no clearly correct moral answer

# 7. Reading

Read.

Ethicists have long debated whether actions should be evaluated according to intentions or consequences. Consequentialist theories argue that the morality of an action depends primarily on the results it produces. If an action creates the greatest overall benefit, it is considered morally justified.

By contrast, deontological approaches maintain that certain actions remain inherently right or wrong regardless of their outcomes. According to this perspective, principles such as honesty, fairness and respect for human dignity should not be sacrificed simply because doing so might produce desirable consequences.

In reality, many ethical decisions involve elements of both approaches. People often consider intentions, foreseeable consequences and proportional responsibility before reaching a moral judgement.
