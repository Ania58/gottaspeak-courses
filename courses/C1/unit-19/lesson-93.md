## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:

- express complex opinions about morality and human behaviour

## Case A – The Silent Team

A project manager presents an idea that contains a serious mistake.

Everyone in the meeting notices it.

Nobody says anything because nobody wants to embarrass the manager.

The project later fails.

---

## Case B – The Group Chat

Several colleagues begin mocking another employee in a private group chat.

Nobody joins the conversation, but nobody asks them to stop either.

---

## Case C – The New Employee

A new employee notices that small company expenses are regularly reported dishonestly.

Everyone says,

*"That's simply how things are done here."*

---

## Case D – The Emergency

A man collapses in a crowded train station.

Hundreds of people see him.

Most assume that someone else has already called for help.

Several minutes pass before anyone acts.

---

## Discussion

2. Why do people stay silent?
3. Does doing nothing make someone partly responsible?
4. Which case would be the hardest to handle?

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- diffusion of responsibility – tendency for individuals to feel less responsible when others are present
- conformity bias – tendency to adopt the behaviour or opinions of a group
- moral disengagement – psychological process of distancing oneself from ethical responsibility
- bystander effect – reduced likelihood of helping when others are also present
- normative social influence – pressure to conform in order to gain acceptance or avoid rejection
- ethical blind spot – failure to recognise the moral implications of a situation
- collective responsibility – shared responsibility among members of a group
- moral courage – willingness to act ethically despite personal risk
- cognitive dissonance – psychological discomfort caused by conflicting beliefs and actions
- rationalisation – creating acceptable explanations for questionable behaviour

# 7. Reading

Read.

Psychologists have repeatedly demonstrated that human behaviour is strongly influenced by social context. Individuals who consider themselves honest and compassionate may still behave unethically when faced with group pressure, authority or uncertainty.

One well-known phenomenon is the bystander effect, in which people are less likely to offer assistance when many others are present. Responsibility becomes psychologically distributed across the group, making each individual feel less personally accountable.

Researchers have also identified processes such as conformity bias and moral disengagement. These mechanisms allow people to justify behaviour that conflicts with their personal values without perceiving themselves as unethical.

These findings suggest that ethical behaviour depends not only on character but also on circumstances, self-awareness and the willingness to act independently.
