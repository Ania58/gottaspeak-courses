# Lesson 61 – Culture Shock

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:
- discuss cultural differences and adaptation naturally
- talk about misunderstandings and stereotypes
- describe emotional reactions to living abroad
- reflect on identity and belonging

---

# 1. Story Starter

Read the story.

When Isabella moved from Spain to Sweden for work, she expected a few differences. What she didn't expect was how exhausting those differences would feel.

In Spain, she was used to spontaneous plans, long conversations and lively social gatherings. In Sweden, however, she found people much more reserved. Colleagues rarely interrupted each other, lunches were quieter and everyone seemed to value personal space.

At first, Isabella interpreted this as coldness and thought people simply didn't like her. She began to feel lonely and even considered moving back home.

Over time, however, she realised that her colleagues were not unfriendly. They simply expressed kindness differently. Invitations took longer, but relationships became deeper. People respected boundaries and valued honesty.

Three years later, Sweden feels like home, and Isabella laughs when she remembers how shocked she was during her first months.

# 3. Language From the Story

Work with these expressions.

- culture shock – confusion or discomfort caused by unfamiliar customs
- adapt to something – become used to a new situation
- personal space – physical and emotional distance people prefer
- stereotype – simplified belief about a group of people
- misunderstanding – failure to understand someone correctly
- reserved – not openly expressive or emotional
- social norms – accepted ways of behaving
- homesickness – missing home and familiar surroundings
- sense of belonging – feeling accepted and connected
- adjustment period – time needed to adapt

# 7. Reading

Read.

Culture shock is a normal part of adapting to a new environment. It often begins with excitement, followed by frustration, confusion and homesickness.

Many misunderstandings occur because people assume that their own customs and habits are universal. In reality, different cultures simply have different ways of expressing politeness, friendship and respect.

Over time, most people gradually learn to navigate these differences. In many cases, adapting to another culture not only changes how people see others, but also how they see themselves.

Living abroad can therefore become a source of personal growth and a deeper understanding of the world.
