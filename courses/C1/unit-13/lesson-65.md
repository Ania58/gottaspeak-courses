# Lesson 65 – Home: A Place or a Feeling?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:
- discuss the meaning of home and belonging naturally
- talk about identity, roots and nostalgia
- express complex emotions about moving and change
- reflect on what makes people feel connected to a place

---

# 1. Story Starter

Read the story.

When Daniel left Poland at the age of twenty-five, he thought he would stay abroad for only a few years.

At first, everything felt exciting. New people, a new language and a completely different lifestyle made every day feel like an adventure.

Years passed.

He built a career, made friends and eventually started a family. Yet whenever someone asked him where home was, he hesitated.

Poland still felt like home because of his childhood memories, family traditions and language.

At the same time, the country where he had spent most of his adult life also felt like home.

Strangely, whenever he visited Poland, some things felt unfamiliar. But when he returned abroad, he missed Poland again.

Eventually, Daniel stopped trying to choose between the two places.

He realised that home was no longer a single location but a combination of people, memories and feelings.

# 3. Language From the Story

Work with these expressions.

- sense of belonging – feeling accepted and connected
- roots – connection with family, culture and origins
- nostalgia – longing for the past
- identity – understanding of who you are
- familiarity – feeling that something is known and comfortable
- homesickness – missing home and familiar surroundings
- emotional attachment – strong connection to something
- memories – experiences remembered from the past
- community – people who share connections and support
- comfort zone – familiar and safe environment

# 7. Reading

Read.

People often think of home as a physical place. However, psychologists suggest that home is also connected to identity, relationships and memories.

For many people who have lived abroad, the idea of home becomes increasingly complex. They may feel attached to more than one country and experience a strange mixture of familiarity and distance when returning to places from their past.

This phenomenon is sometimes called reverse culture shock. People discover that while places may remain the same, they themselves have changed.

As a result, home often becomes less about geography and more about belonging, meaning and emotional connection.
