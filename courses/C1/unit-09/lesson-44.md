## 4. Grammar – Being Diplomatic

Diplomatic English often softens disagreement while keeping the message clear.

Compare:

Too direct:
- You're wrong.

More diplomatic:
- I'm not sure I see it quite the same way.

Too blunt:
- I can't do it.

More natural:
- I'd love to help, but I don't think I'll be able to.

Too aggressive:
- This is unacceptable.

More constructive:
- I'm afraid I'm not completely satisfied with this.

---

### Useful Language

Polite disagreement:

- I see your point, but…  
- I'm not entirely convinced that…  
- I can understand why you feel that way, although…  
- That's one way of looking at it, but…  

Polite refusal:

- I'd rather not…  
- I don't think I'm the right person for that  
- I'm afraid I won't be able to…  
- I appreciate the offer, but…  

Constructive criticism:

- One thing that might help is…  
- Perhaps it would work better if…  
- Have you considered…?  
- I wonder if…

## 7. Reading

Read the text.

Many people associate disagreement with conflict and therefore avoid difficult conversations entirely. However, avoiding confrontation does not necessarily protect relationships. In some cases, it creates resentment and misunderstanding.

Diplomatic communication involves expressing disagreement or dissatisfaction without humiliating or attacking the other person. The goal is not to avoid honesty, but to communicate it constructively.

Interestingly, people who struggle to say no often fear disappointing others or appearing selfish. Yet constant agreement may eventually damage relationships if frustration remains unspoken.

Healthy communication depends not only on kindness, but also on clarity and boundaries.
