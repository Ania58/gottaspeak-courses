# Lesson 96 – Why Do People Judge Others So Quickly?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:

- discuss how first impressions influence judgement
- analyse stereotypes and unconscious bias
- evaluate the fairness of snap judgements
- express nuanced opinions about social perception

Read each situation.

Your task:

- Explain your first impression.
- Decide whether it is fair.
- Predict what really happened.

---

## Situation 1 – The Interview

A candidate arrives ten minutes late to a job interview.

They look stressed and apologise briefly before sitting down.

Would your opinion of them change immediately?

---

## Situation 2 – The Restaurant

A customer speaks impatiently to a waiter and appears rude.

Ten minutes later you discover they have just received terrible personal news.

Should your judgement change?

---

## Situation 3 – The Classroom

A student rarely speaks during lessons.

Many classmates assume they are unfriendly or uninterested.

Later everyone discovers that the student recently moved from another country and lacks confidence in speaking English.

---

## Situation 4 – The Neighbour

A new neighbour never smiles or says hello.

People in the building begin describing them as arrogant.

Months later they learn that the neighbour suffers from severe social anxiety.

---

## Situation 5 – The Office

A colleague always leaves work exactly at 5 p.m.

Others believe they are lazy and uncommitted.

Eventually they find out the colleague is the sole carer for an elderly parent.

---

## Discussion

1. Which judgement was the least fair?
2. Why do people often jump to conclusions?
3. Should we wait before forming opinions?
4. How often are first impressions correct?

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- implicit bias – unconscious attitudes that influence our judgement without us realising it
- snap judgement – opinion formed almost instantly with very little information
- preconceived notion – opinion formed before knowing the facts
- attribution error – tendency to explain someone's behaviour by their personality rather than the situation
- social perception – process of interpreting other people's behaviour
- unconscious prejudice – hidden bias that affects attitudes and decisions
- cognitive shortcut – mental strategy that simplifies complex decisions but may reduce accuracy
- social categorisation – automatic process of placing people into social groups
- stereotype threat – anxiety caused by the fear of confirming a negative stereotype
- confirmation bias – tendency to notice information that supports existing beliefs while ignoring evidence that contradicts them

# 7. Reading

Read.

Human beings naturally form first impressions within seconds of meeting someone. Psychologists believe this rapid evaluation developed because quick decisions helped our ancestors identify potential threats and opportunities.

Although these mental shortcuts can be useful, they often lead to systematic errors. One common example is the attribution error, where people explain another person's behaviour by referring to personality rather than considering external circumstances. Similarly, implicit biases influence our expectations even when we genuinely believe we are being objective.

Research suggests that becoming aware of these biases does not eliminate them completely. However, slowing down our judgement, actively seeking additional information and questioning our initial assumptions can significantly improve the fairness of our decisions.
