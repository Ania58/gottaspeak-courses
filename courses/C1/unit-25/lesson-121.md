# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- herd mentality – tendency to adopt the behaviour or opinions of a larger group without independently evaluating them
- normative social influence – pressure to conform in order to gain acceptance or avoid rejection by others
- informational social influence – tendency to rely on the behaviour or opinions of others when uncertain about what is correct
- groupthink – pattern of decision-making in which the desire for harmony suppresses critical evaluation and dissent
- social conditioning – process through which beliefs and behaviours are shaped by cultural and social expectations
- independent judgement – ability to evaluate situations using one's own reasoning despite external pressure
- dissent – expression of disagreement with a commonly accepted opinion or decision
- social conformity – adjustment of behaviour or beliefs to align with those of a group
- pluralistic ignorance – situation in which individuals privately disagree with a group but mistakenly assume everyone else agrees
- bystander effect – tendency for individuals to be less likely to act when others are present

# 7. Reading

Read.

Humans are highly social creatures. Throughout history, belonging to a group increased the chances of survival, making cooperation an essential evolutionary advantage. As a result, people often adapt their behaviour to match those around them, even when they privately disagree.

Psychologists distinguish between two main forms of social influence. Normative influence occurs when people conform to gain approval or avoid rejection. Informational influence, on the other hand, occurs when individuals assume that others possess more accurate knowledge, particularly in unfamiliar situations.

Although conformity can promote cooperation and social stability, excessive conformity may discourage creativity, suppress dissent and contribute to poor decision-making. Healthy societies therefore depend not only on cooperation but also on individuals who are willing to question assumptions and express unpopular opinions when necessary.
