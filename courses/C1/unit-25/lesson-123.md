# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- habit formation – gradual process through which repeated behaviours become automatic responses to specific situations
- behavioural reinforcement – strengthening of behaviour through rewards or positive consequences
- self-regulation – ability to monitor and control one's thoughts, emotions and behaviour in pursuit of long-term goals
- delayed gratification – willingness to postpone immediate rewards in order to obtain greater future benefits
- impulse control – ability to resist immediate urges despite temptation
- behavioural trigger – event, emotion or situation that automatically initiates a particular behaviour
- automaticity – state in which a behaviour is performed with little or no conscious effort
- conditioned behaviour – learned response repeatedly associated with specific circumstances
- behavioural intervention – deliberate strategy designed to modify established patterns of behaviour
- self-discipline – consistent ability to act according to long-term objectives rather than temporary emotions

# 6. Reading

Read.

Habits allow the brain to conserve mental energy by transforming repeated actions into automatic routines. Once a behaviour becomes habitual, it requires considerably less conscious effort than when it was first learned. Although this efficiency is beneficial, it also explains why undesirable habits can be remarkably persistent.

Researchers suggest that habits typically consist of three elements: a trigger, a routine and a reward. Over time, the brain begins to anticipate the reward whenever the trigger appears, making the behaviour increasingly automatic. Consequently, simply relying on motivation is often insufficient to change established routines.

Successful behavioural change usually involves modifying the environment rather than depending solely on willpower. Removing distractions, creating consistent routines and introducing small, manageable goals are generally more effective than attempting dramatic overnight transformations.
