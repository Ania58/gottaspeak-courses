# 6. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- status quo bias – tendency to prefer existing situations simply because they are familiar, even when better alternatives exist
- uncertainty tolerance – ability to remain psychologically comfortable despite incomplete information or unpredictable outcomes
- cognitive rigidity – difficulty adapting one's thinking when circumstances or evidence change
- behavioural adaptation – process of modifying behaviour in response to changing environments or challenges
- psychological resilience – capacity to recover from setbacks and adapt positively to adversity
- risk aversion – tendency to avoid options involving uncertainty, even when potential rewards are significant
- transformative change – profound change that fundamentally alters beliefs, behaviour or circumstances
- comfort-zone mentality – preference for familiar experiences that limits exploration and personal development
- adaptive mindset – willingness to adjust one's thinking and behaviour in response to new situations
- loss aversion – tendency to perceive potential losses as psychologically more significant than equivalent gains

# 8. Reading

Read.

Although people often describe themselves as either "risk-takers" or "risk-averse", psychological research suggests that our attitude towards change depends heavily on context. Individuals may willingly embrace change in one area of life while resisting it strongly in another.

One explanation is known as **loss aversion**. Research indicates that people tend to experience the emotional impact of losing something more intensely than the pleasure of gaining something of equal value. Consequently, even beneficial changes may feel threatening because they involve uncertainty and the possibility of loss.

Fortunately, adaptability is not a fixed personality trait. By repeatedly facing manageable challenges, reflecting on previous successes and learning from setbacks, individuals can gradually increase both their resilience and their tolerance for uncertainty. Rather than eliminating fear, successful adaptation involves acting despite it.
