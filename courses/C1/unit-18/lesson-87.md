# Lesson 87 – Should We Always Be Available?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:
- discuss digital communication habits naturally
- analyse expectations surrounding availability
- reflect on work-life boundaries
- express opinions about healthy communication

---

# 1. Three People, Three Habits

Read the situations.

Your task:

- Decide who has the healthiest relationship with technology.
- Explain the advantages and disadvantages of each approach.
- Discuss what you would do differently.

---

## Case A – Mia

Mia replies to almost every message within a few minutes.

She feels guilty if someone has to wait for a reply, even during evenings or weekends.

---

## Case B – Ethan

Ethan checks messages only three times a day.

His friends sometimes complain that he replies too slowly, but he says he feels much less stressed.

---

## Case C – Laura

Laura answers work emails late at night and often responds during holidays.

She worries that saying "no" might damage her career.

---

## Discussion

1. Which person has the healthiest habits?
2. Is replying quickly a sign of respect?
3. Should people be available outside working hours?
4. Have expectations changed over the last ten years?

Work with these expressions.

- set boundaries – establish clear limits
- be constantly available – always be ready to respond
- disconnect from work – stop thinking about work during free time
- respect someone's time – recognise that another person's time is valuable
- urgent request – something requiring immediate attention
- unrealistic expectation – demand that is unreasonable
- digital etiquette – accepted behaviour when communicating online
- response time – time it takes to reply
- work-life boundary – separation between work and personal life

# 6. Reading

Read.

Smartphones and messaging apps have transformed communication by making it possible to contact almost anyone at any time. While this offers convenience, it has also created new social expectations.

Many people now feel pressure to respond immediately, even when they are relaxing or spending time with family. In some workplaces, employees feel uncomfortable ignoring emails after working hours because they fear appearing uncommitted.

Researchers argue that constantly being available can increase stress and reduce recovery time. For this reason, several countries have introduced or discussed a "right to disconnect," allowing employees to ignore work communications outside official working hours.
