# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- convenience culture – a lifestyle centred on speed and ease rather than effort
- behavioural dependency – reliance on certain behaviours or technologies to function normally
- cognitive offloading – transferring mental tasks to technology instead of doing them yourself
- unintended consequence – unexpected result of a decision or innovation
- diminishing returns – point at which additional benefits become progressively smaller
- technological mediation – technology acting as an intermediary between people and everyday activities
- erosion of practical skills – gradual loss of everyday abilities through lack of use
- frictionless experience – process designed to remove effort, delays or obstacles
- decision fatigue – reduced ability to make good decisions after making many choices
- perceived efficiency – belief that something is more efficient, whether or not it actually is

# 7. Reading

Read.

Many technological innovations are designed to reduce effort and increase efficiency. Automation has transformed everyday life by simplifying tasks that once required significant time and energy.

However, some researchers argue that excessive convenience may produce unintended consequences. As people rely increasingly on digital tools, they may gradually lose practical skills or become less confident solving problems independently.

Another concern is cognitive offloading. By allowing technology to remember information, calculate routes and organise schedules, people may reduce the amount of mental effort they invest in everyday tasks.

Supporters of automation respond that technology frees people to focus on more meaningful activities. Critics counter that convenience should enhance human abilities rather than replace them.
