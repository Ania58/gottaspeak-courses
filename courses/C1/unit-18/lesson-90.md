# Lesson 90 – Would You Disconnect for One Month?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:

- discuss digital dependence and digital detoxes
- analyse the psychological effects of constant connectivity
- evaluate the role of technology in everyday life
- express well-developed opinions about balance and wellbeing

---

# 1. The Challenge

Imagine you volunteer for a research project.

For **30 days** you must give up:

- social media
- streaming platforms
- online shopping
- AI assistants
- video games
- non-essential smartphone use

You may still:

- make phone calls
- send SMS messages
- use maps in emergencies
- use a computer for work or study

If you complete the challenge, you receive **€10,000**.

---

## Discussion

1. Would you accept the challenge?
2. Which restriction would be the most difficult?
3. Which habit would you miss the least?
4. Would the money influence your decision?

# 2. Four Participants

Read the experiences.

- Decide who adapted best.

---

## Participant A – Emma

The first week was extremely difficult.

She constantly reached for her phone without thinking.

By the third week she was reading books again and sleeping better.

---

## Participant B – Daniel

He enjoyed the first few days but quickly became bored.

---

She found the challenge surprisingly easy.

3. Is technology dependence similar to other habits?
4. Could you permanently reduce your screen time?

# 3. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- compulsive behaviour – repetitive behaviour that is difficult to control
- habitual checking – automatic tendency to check something repeatedly
- intermittent reinforcement – unpredictable rewards that strengthen a behaviour
- cognitive resilience – ability to maintain effective thinking under pressure or distraction
- attentional fragmentation – reduced concentration caused by frequent interruptions
- digital dependency – psychological reliance on digital technology
- behavioural conditioning – process through which repeated experiences shape behaviour
- intentional living – making conscious rather than automatic choices
- stimulus control – changing your environment to reduce unwanted habits
- psychological withdrawal – emotional discomfort experienced after giving something up

# 7. Reading

Read.

Digital technologies have transformed communication, entertainment and productivity. Yet psychologists increasingly question whether constant connectivity comes at a psychological cost.

Many digital platforms are intentionally designed to maximise user engagement through personalised recommendations, endless scrolling and variable rewards. These mechanisms encourage habitual checking and make disengagement surprisingly difficult.

Studies suggest that reducing unnecessary screen time may improve concentration, sleep quality and subjective wellbeing. However, researchers also emphasise that lasting change rarely comes from temporary abstinence alone. Sustainable improvements usually require new routines, greater self-awareness and deliberate control over digital habits.

Rather than rejecting technology altogether, many experts argue that people should learn to use it intentionally instead of automatically.
