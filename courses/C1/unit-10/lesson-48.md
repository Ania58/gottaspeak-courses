# Lesson 48 – Office Politics and Difficult Workplaces

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:
- discuss workplace conflict and office politics naturally  
- describe professional tension and difficult dynamics  
- raise concerns diplomatically  
- analyse workplace situations and suggest solutions  

---

# 1. Workplace Case Study

Read the situation.

Sophie works in a marketing department with six colleagues. Over the past few months, tension has started building within the team.

Her manager, Daniel, appears unusually close to one employee, Jason. Jason often receives the most visible projects and is regularly praised during meetings, even when others have contributed equally. Meanwhile, mistakes made by Jason are quietly overlooked, whereas similar mistakes by other employees are discussed publicly.

Recently, Sophie discovered through informal conversations that Jason had already been told about an upcoming promotion before the rest of the team had even heard about the opportunity.

As frustration grew, gossip started spreading. Some colleagues began complaining privately while acting perfectly friendly during meetings. Others started keeping their distance and avoiding collaboration.

Sophie feels increasingly uncomfortable. She dislikes confrontation but worries that the atmosphere is becoming toxic and damaging team morale.

# 3. Language From the Situation

Work with these expressions from the case.

- office politics – power dynamics and unofficial influence at work  
- favouritism – treating someone more positively than others unfairly  
- workplace tension – uncomfortable or difficult atmosphere  
- undermine someone – weaken someone's confidence or position  
- mixed signals – unclear or contradictory behaviour  
- professional reputation – how someone is viewed professionally  
- passive-aggressive behaviour – indirect hostility  
- toxic atmosphere – unhealthy working environment  
- workplace morale – team motivation and emotional climate  
- behind-the-scenes decisions – decisions made informally or secretly

# 4. Workplace Communication Toolkit

## Raising Concerns Professionally

Instead of sounding accusatory:

Too direct:
- This is unfair.

More professional:
- I'm slightly concerned about how this is being handled.

Too emotional:
- Management clearly has favourites.

More diplomatic:
- I've noticed some concerns about consistency within the team.

---

## Useful Workplace Language

### Raising concerns

- I wanted to bring something up  
- I've noticed that…  
- I'm slightly concerned that…  
- It might be worth discussing…  
- I may be mistaken, but…  

### Disagreeing professionally

- From my perspective…  
- I see it somewhat differently  
- I'm not sure everyone sees it that way  
- I don't want to jump to conclusions, but…  
- There may be more to the situation than…

# 7. Reading – Case Resolution

Read.

Workplace conflict often becomes more damaging when concerns remain unspoken. Employees may avoid difficult conversations to protect relationships or avoid appearing confrontational.

However, silence can unintentionally reinforce unhealthy dynamics. Gossip and frustration frequently grow in environments where communication feels unsafe or inconsistent.

Experts in workplace communication suggest addressing concerns early and professionally. While difficult conversations may feel uncomfortable initially, they often prevent larger problems from developing later.

Healthy workplaces are not conflict-free. Rather, they are environments where concerns can be discussed respectfully and transparently.
