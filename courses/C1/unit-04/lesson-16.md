## 4. Grammar – Advanced Concession 

We express contrast without simplifying the idea:

- **Much as** teenagers may appear independent, their decision-making systems are still developing.  
- **While it would be inaccurate to claim that** teenagers are irrational, they are more influenced by emotional stimuli.  
- **Although teenagers are capable of logical reasoning**, they may not consistently apply it under pressure.  
- **Even though they understand consequences**, they may still prioritise immediate reward.  

---

### Task 1 – Transformation

Rewrite using advanced concession:

1. Teenagers seem independent, but they are not fully developed  
2. They understand risks, but they still take them  
3. They can think logically, but emotions affect them  
4. They know consequences, but they ignore them  
5. They try to control themselves, but they fail  

---

## 5. Grammar – Limitation & Scope 

We define how far a statement is true:

- **To the extent that** the brain is still developing, behaviour may be inconsistent  
- **Insofar as** emotional systems dominate, rational control is reduced  
- **Partly because of** neurological development, teenagers are more reactive

## 8. Reading

Read the text.

The teenage brain is often described as a work in progress. While many cognitive abilities are already in place, the systems responsible for regulation and long-term planning continue to develop well into early adulthood.

What is particularly significant is the imbalance between emotional reactivity and cognitive control. Teenagers are highly responsive to reward, feedback and social approval, yet their ability to regulate these responses is not fully established.

As a result, behaviour may appear inconsistent. Decisions that seem rational in one context may be replaced by impulsive reactions in another, especially under emotional pressure.

Understanding this developmental stage is essential. Rather than viewing teenage behaviour as irrational, it may be more accurate to see it as incomplete.
