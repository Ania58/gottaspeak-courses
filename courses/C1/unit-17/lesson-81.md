# Lesson 81 – Why Do We Procrastinate?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:
- discuss procrastination and motivation naturally
- analyse why people delay important tasks
- compare different working styles
- express opinions about productivity and self-discipline

---

# 1. The Investigation

Three people all struggle to finish important tasks.

Your task:

- Decide why each person procrastinates.
- Suggest practical solutions.
- Decide whose situation is easiest to improve.

---

## Case A – Sophie

Sophie always waits until the last possible moment.

She insists that she "works better under pressure," although she often feels stressed and exhausted afterwards.

---

## Case B – Daniel

Daniel spends hours planning every detail before starting.

He wants everything to be perfect, so he rarely actually begins the project.

---

## Case C – Emily

Emily starts working, but every few minutes she checks social media, replies to messages or watches another short video.

At the end of the day, she feels busy but has achieved very little.

---

## Discussion

1. Which person do you relate to most?
2. Why do people procrastinate?
3. Is procrastination laziness?
4. Can pressure sometimes improve performance?

# 2. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- put something off – delay doing something
- last-minute rush – period of intense activity just before a deadline
- perfection paralysis – inability to act because you want everything to be perfect
- get sidetracked – become distracted from what you intended to do
- attention span – length of time you can concentrate
- deadline pressure – stress caused by limited time
- avoid discomfort – stay away from unpleasant feelings or tasks
- build momentum – gradually become more productive once you start
- mental block – psychological difficulty that prevents progress
- productive procrastination – doing less important tasks to avoid the important one

# 6. Reading

Read.

Many people believe procrastination is simply a sign of laziness, but psychologists suggest something different.

Research shows that procrastination is often linked to emotions rather than poor time management. Difficult, boring or uncertain tasks can trigger anxiety or self-doubt, making people temporarily avoid them.

Ironically, avoiding the task usually increases stress instead of reducing it.

Experts suggest that starting with a very small step is often enough to build momentum. Once people begin working, the task usually feels much less intimidating than they expected.
