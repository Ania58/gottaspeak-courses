# Lesson 82 – Why Do Smart People Make Bad Decisions?

## 🎯 Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:
- discuss decision-making naturally
- analyse how emotions influence choices
- identify common thinking mistakes
- express opinions about judgement and rationality

---

# 1. Three Decisions

Read the situations.

Your task:

- Decide what went wrong.
- Explain why each person made that decision.
- Suggest what they could have done differently.

---

## Case A – Alex

Alex spent weeks researching which laptop to buy.

After reading hundreds of reviews, he became overwhelmed and still couldn't decide.

In the end, he bought nothing.

---

## Case B – Olivia

Olivia accepted a job offer immediately because the interviewer seemed friendly and confident.

Three months later, she realised the company was completely different from what she had expected.

---

## Case C – Ryan

Ryan invested all his savings in a company because everyone online said it was "guaranteed to succeed."

A few months later, he lost most of his money.

---

## Discussion

1. Which mistake seems most understandable?
2. Why do people ignore warning signs?
3. Can too much information make decisions harder?
4. Have you ever regretted a quick decision?

# 2. Language From the Topic

Work with these expressions.

- analysis paralysis – inability to decide because of excessive thinking
- gut feeling – instinctive opinion without conscious reasoning
- confirmation bias – tendency to notice information that supports existing beliefs
- jump to conclusions – form an opinion too quickly
- second-guess yourself – repeatedly doubt your own decisions
- overlook something – fail to notice something important
- weigh up the options – carefully compare different possibilities
- snap judgement – opinion formed almost immediately
- cloud your judgement – make it harder to think clearly
- hindsight bias – believing something was obvious only after it happened

# 6. Reading

Read.

People often assume that intelligent individuals naturally make better decisions. However, psychologists have found that intelligence does not protect us from poor judgement.

Our decisions are influenced by emotions, habits, previous experiences and cognitive biases. Even experts can ignore evidence that contradicts what they already believe or become overwhelmed when faced with too many options.

Good decision-makers are not people who never make mistakes. Instead, they recognise their thinking errors, remain open to new information and are willing to change their minds when necessary.
