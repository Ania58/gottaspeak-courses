## 4. Grammar – Advanced Conditionals (Real-world Risk)

We use conditionals to describe consequences and risks:

- If people don’t verify information, they may fall victim to scams  
- If she had questioned the caller, she wouldn’t have transferred the money  
- Had he taken a moment to think, he might have avoided the mistake  
- If something sounds urgent, it doesn’t necessarily mean it is real  

---

### Task 1 – Transformation

Rewrite:

1. People trust too quickly → they get scammed  
2. He didn’t check → he lost money  
3. She reacted immediately → she made a mistake  
4. They believed the story → they were manipulated  
5. He didn’t stop → he didn’t realise

## 5. Grammar – Persuasive Framing

Scammers frame messages to influence decisions:

- “You must act now” → urgency  
- “This is your final warning” → fear  
- “We need you to confirm immediately” → authority + pressure

## 8. Reading

Read the text.

Scams are rarely successful because of complexity. In most cases, they work because they exploit basic human reactions. Fear, urgency and trust are powerful forces that can override rational thinking.

A typical scam follows a predictable pattern. First, it establishes credibility, often by impersonating an authority figure. Then, it introduces a problem that requires immediate attention. Finally, it pressures the victim into acting quickly, leaving no time for verification.

What makes these situations particularly dangerous is that they feel real. The emotional response is immediate, and by the time doubt appears, it is often too late.

Understanding these patterns is the first step in protecting oneself.
