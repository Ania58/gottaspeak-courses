## Idioms Focus: Emotions and Social Situations (7 min)

Idioms are very common in everyday English, especially when people talk about **feelings, relationships and social interactions**.  
At B1 level, it is important to understand their **meaning and use**, even if you don’t use them actively all the time.

### Key idioms:

- **break the ice** – to make people feel more relaxed in a new or uncomfortable situation  
  > A bit of humour helped break the ice at the meeting.

- **be on the same wavelength** – to understand each other well  
  > We work well together because we’re on the same wavelength.

- **get something off your chest** – to talk about something that has been worrying you  
  > She needed to get something off her chest.

- **be fed up with** – to feel annoyed or tired of a situation  
  > I’m fed up with working late every day.

- **under the weather** – to feel slightly ill or not well  
  > He didn’t come to work because he was feeling under the weather.

## Reading: Saying What You Really Feel (8 min)

Many adults find it difficult to talk openly about their emotions. In social situations, people often try to appear confident and relaxed, even when they feel uncomfortable or stressed. This can make it harder to build honest relationships.

Sometimes, a simple conversation is enough to break the ice and help people feel more at ease. When individuals are on the same wavelength, communication becomes more natural. However, when feelings are ignored for too long, frustration can grow.

Getting something off your chest can be uncomfortable, but it often leads to relief and stronger connections. People who feel supported are more likely to communicate honestly and deal with challenges in a healthier way.
