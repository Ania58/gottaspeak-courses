## Language Focus – Parts of a Good Apology

A clear apology usually has **three elements**:

### 1️⃣ Acknowledging the problem

- I realise this caused a problem.  
- I understand why you’re upset.  
- I can see that this affected your work.

---

### 2️⃣ Taking responsibility

- I should have checked it earlier.  
- I shouldn’t have ignored the warning signs.  
- That was my mistake.

---

### 3️⃣ Offering a solution

- I’ll fix this immediately.  
- Let me sort this out.  
- I’ll make sure this doesn’t happen again.

A weak apology often **avoids responsibility**:

- “Mistakes were made.”  
- “There was a misunderstanding.”

## Idioms & Phrasal Verbs

- **mess something up** – make a mistake  
- **own up (to something)** – admit responsibility  
- **make up for something** – compensate for a mistake  
- **sort something out** – solve a problem  
- **smooth things over** – repair a relationship after conflict  

---

## Reading – The Email That Fixed Everything

When Oliver sent the wrong financial report to a client, he realised immediately that he had messed things up. The document contained outdated figures, and the client had already started reviewing it.

Instead of ignoring the problem, Oliver decided to own up to his mistake. He sent a message explaining the situation clearly and apologised for the confusion.

“I should have double-checked the file before sending it,” he wrote. “That was my responsibility.”

He then attached the correct version and offered to walk the client through the updated numbers.

The client appreciated the honesty. Problems happen in every organisation, but the way people deal with them can rebuild trust or destroy it. In this case, Oliver managed to smooth things over and even strengthen the relationship.
