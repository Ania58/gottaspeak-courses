## Language Focus – Negotiating Solutions

When people try to solve problems together, they often:

- suggest solutions  
- offer compromises  
- set conditions  

A useful structure for this is the **first conditional**.

### ➤ First Conditional for Solutions

We use it to talk about **real possible results**.

Structure:

**If + present simple → will + base verb**

Examples:

- If you send the updated file today, we **will review it immediately**.
- If we adjust the deadline, the team **will have enough time**.
- If you explain the situation clearly, they **will understand**.

This structure is very common in:
- negotiations
- problem-solving
- customer service
- workplace discussions

- **back down** – stop arguing or defending a position  
- **sort something out** – resolve a problem

## Reading – A Difficult Conversation

Two departments in a company were arguing about project deadlines. The marketing team wanted to launch the campaign quickly, but the design team said they needed more time to finish the visuals.

The discussion became tense during a meeting. At first, both sides defended their position strongly. No one wanted to back down.

Finally, the project manager suggested a compromise. “If the design team finishes the main visuals by Friday, the marketing team will delay the campaign by three days,” she said.

The proposal seemed reasonable. The design team agreed to work late that week, and the marketing team promised to adjust their schedule.

In the end, both sides had to meet halfway. The project was launched a little later than planned, but the result was much stronger.

## Problem-Solving Task – Finding a Compromise

Read the situation:

A team member missed an important deadline.  
The manager is unhappy, but the employee says the workload was unrealistic.

Propose **two possible compromises** using the structure:

- If __________, we will __________.

Example:

If we redistribute the tasks, we will avoid delays next time.
