## Vocabulary Focus: Reporting Verbs and Attitude

Reporting verbs help us show **how** something was said and **what attitude** the speaker had.

### Key reporting verbs

- **claim** – say something is true, without proof  
- **admit** – accept that something was wrong or true  
- **deny** – say something is not true  
- **suggest** – mention an idea or possibility  
- **explain** – give reasons or details  
- **warn** – tell someone about possible danger  
- **promise** – say you will do something  

### Common patterns

- claim / admit / deny **that**  
- suggest **that**  
- warn **someone about**  
- promise **to + verb**

## Reading: Reporting Reactions to Public Statements

When public statements are reported in the media, the choice of reporting verb is rarely neutral. Different verbs communicate different attitudes and levels of certainty. For example, when a spokesperson claims something, it suggests that the information has not been fully proven. On the other hand, when someone admits a mistake, it often signals responsibility and transparency.

Journalists carefully select reporting verbs to reflect the speaker’s position. A warning implies concern about future consequences, while a promise suggests commitment to action. These subtle differences influence how readers interpret events and decide whom to trust.

In official reports, verbs such as *deny* or *explain* are frequently used to balance opposing viewpoints. This allows media outlets to present multiple perspectives without directly supporting one side. However, readers should be aware that these choices still shape interpretation.

Understanding reporting verbs helps readers recognise attitude and intention. It also improves their ability to report information accurately when speaking or writing themselves.
