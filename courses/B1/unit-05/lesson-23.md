## Idioms Focus: Emotions and Reactions (8 min)

Idioms are often used to talk about **feelings, tension and emotional reactions** in everyday English.

- **get on someone’s nerves** – annoy someone  
  > Loud noises really get on my nerves.

- **be on the same wavelength** – understand each other well  
  > We work well together because we’re on the same wavelength.

- **keep something to yourself** – not share thoughts or feelings  
  > He prefers to keep his worries to himself.

- **lose your temper** – become angry  
  > She lost her temper during the argument.

- **bottle things up** – hide emotions instead of expressing them  
  > Bottling things up can cause stress.

## Reading: Understanding Emotions in Relationships (16–18 min)

Emotions play a central role in how people communicate and connect. When feelings are expressed clearly, relationships tend to be more balanced and supportive. However, many adults struggle to communicate emotions openly, especially when conversations feel uncomfortable.

Some people prefer to keep things to themselves, believing that silence prevents conflict. Others bottle things up until frustration builds and emotions suddenly explode. Losing your temper may bring short-term relief, but it often damages trust and understanding.

Healthy communication requires emotional awareness. Being on the same wavelength does not mean always agreeing, but recognising each other’s feelings. When people listen carefully and respond calmly, even difficult conversations can strengthen relationships.

Learning how to manage emotional reactions helps people avoid unnecessary conflict. Expressing feelings honestly—without blame or anger—creates space for empathy, respect and deeper connection.
