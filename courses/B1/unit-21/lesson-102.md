## Grammar Focus – Describing Gradual Change

English often uses **comparative repetition** to show that something is **changing step by step**.

### ➤ Comparative repetition

Structure:

**comparative + and + comparative**

Examples:

- The situation is getting **better and better**.  
- Traffic is becoming **worse and worse**.  
- Life is moving **faster and faster**.

This structure shows **continuous change**.

---

### ➤ get + comparative

Another common way to describe change.

Examples:

- Things are **getting easier**.  
- The problem is **getting worse**.  
- The weather is **getting colder**.

## Vocabulary – Positive and Negative Change

Positive change:

- improvement  
- progress  
- development  
- recovery  

Negative change:

- decline  
- crisis  
- damage  
- pressure  

---

## Idioms & Phrasal Verbs

- **pick up** – improve or increase  
- **slow down** – become slower  
- **speed up** – move faster  
- **build up** – increase gradually  
- **fall apart** – collapse or stop functioning well

## Reading – When a Small Problem Grows

At first, the problem seemed small. A few employees complained about the new scheduling system, but management believed the situation would improve naturally.

However, over the next few weeks, the complaints became louder. The workload was getting heavier and heavier, and employees felt more and more pressure.

Communication between teams also started to break down. Small misunderstandings built up over time, and cooperation became worse and worse.

Eventually, productivity began to fall. Deadlines were missed, and clients noticed the difference. What began as a minor organisational issue slowly turned into a serious workplace problem.

In the end, the company realised that ignoring small issues can allow them to grow into much larger ones.
