## Grammar Focus – No Longer and Not Anymore

We use **no longer** and **not anymore** to say that something **was true before but is not true now**.

### ➤ no longer

Often used in more formal English.

Examples:

- Many people **no longer read printed newspapers**.  
- Some shops **no longer accept cash**.

---

### ➤ not anymore

More common in everyday conversation.

Examples:

- I **don’t watch TV anymore**.  
- They **don’t live here anymore**.

Both expressions describe **change over time**.

## Idioms & Phrasal Verbs

- **die out** – disappear gradually  
- **fade away** – slowly disappear  
- **phase out** – remove something gradually  
- **move away from** – stop using or supporting something  
- **hold on to** – keep something for a long time

## Reading – The End of an Everyday Habit

Only a generation ago, many people started their day by reading a printed newspaper. The morning paper was part of daily life. Families used to discuss headlines at breakfast, and commuters often carried folded newspapers on the train.

Today, this habit is slowly disappearing. Many readers no longer buy physical newspapers because they can read the news instantly on their phones.

Media companies have started to phase out some printed editions and focus more on digital platforms. As technology improves, information spreads faster and faster online.

However, some people still hold on to the traditional experience of reading a paper newspaper. They say it helps them slow down and focus.

Whether printed newspapers will completely disappear or simply become less common remains uncertain.
